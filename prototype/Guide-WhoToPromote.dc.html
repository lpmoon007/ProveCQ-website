Promotion decisions

How to know who to promote — without guessing.

The best individual contributor is not automatically your next great leader. Promoting on past performance is how a six-figure mistake starts. Here is how to bet on proof instead.

The short answer

Promote the person who has already shown the behavior the new role demands — initiative, follow-through under pressure, and the ability to learn fast. Past results tell you what they did in the old job; behavioral proof tells you whether they will deliver in the new one.

Why most promotions go wrong

01

The Peter Principle

People get promoted to the level of their incompetence — because the skills that won the last role are not the skills the next one needs.

02

Recency & halo bias

One recent win or a likeable personality can outweigh months of mixed signal. Gut feel is quietly biased.

03

No test before the bet

You hand over authority first and find out if they can handle it second. Prove reverses that order.

What actually predicts a great promotion

Initiative

Do they already act without being told — before the title makes it their job?

Applied Grit

Do they follow through when the work gets hard and unglamorous?

Learnability

Do they adapt when the demands change, instead of clinging to what worked before?
